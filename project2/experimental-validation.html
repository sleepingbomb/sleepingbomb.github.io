Experimental Validation

Geometric scaling analysis comparing Small, Medium, and Large robot configurations to validate simulation predictions and optimize jumping performance.

Overview

This section documents the experimental validation of our grasshopper-inspired jumping robot. The primary objective was to empirically validate the relationship between link length and locomotion performance through systematic testing.

By varying the geometric scale of the four-bar linkage across three configurations, we observed the trade-offs between stride length, jump height, and kinematic stability.

Test Configurations

Three distinct robot configurations were evaluated, with each iteration created by adding a uniform length increment to every link in the kinematic chain:

Configuration Link A (Base) Link B (Crank) Link C (Coupler) Link D (Leg)
Small (Baseline) 4 cm 4 cm 2 cm 6 cm
Medium (+1 cm) 5 cm 5 cm 3 cm 7 cm
Large (+2 cm) 6 cm 6 cm 4 cm 8 cm

Methodology

Data Acquisition Protocol

  • Execution ‚Äî Robot placed on flat, high-friction surface with Bang-Bang control
  • Video Recording ‚Äî HD camera positioned orthogonal to plane of motion
  • Tracker Analysis ‚Äî Frame-by-frame tracking of "Nose" and "Foot" markers
  • Data Export ‚Äî Coordinate data processed for performance metrics

Performance Metrics (KPIs)

  • Longitudinal Displacement ‚Äî Continuous position x(t) over time
  • Total Distance Traveled ‚Äî Net forward distance after hopping duration
  • Maximum Jump Height ‚Äî Peak vertical displacement (y_max) during flight phase

Key Results

The experimental evaluation revealed a distinct trade-off between longitudinal speed and vertical capability:

Best Longitudinal Displacement

Large Config

Greatest total distance traveled, significantly outperforming baseline. Longer legs increase effective stride length.

Best Jump Height

Medium Config 114 mm

Optimal balance between kinematic reach and motor torque capacity. The "sweet spot" configuration.

Physical Interpretation

  • Small Legs ‚Äî Motors have ample torque, but short links limit vertical range of motion (65 mm jump)
  • Medium Legs ‚Äî Balances kinematic benefit with dynamic motor limitations (114 mm jump)
  • Large Legs ‚Äî Lever arm too long; torque required exceeds motor stall limits (64 mm jump)

Conclusion

üéØ Final Design Recommendation

While the Large configuration offers the best forward velocity, the Medium Configuration provides the most robust overall performance, maximizing obstacle clearance (jump height) while maintaining competitive forward travel.

Future iterations should utilize the Medium geometry unless higher-torque motors are upgraded to support the Large linkage. This validates our simulation predictions and demonstrates the critical importance of actuator-mechanism matching in bio-inspired robotics.
